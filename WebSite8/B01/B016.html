Inhibition

“We had some food and then went to a bar. It was nice to catch up but he just seemed a bit insecure. It’s hard to explain.”

Vincent lit the candle.

“Self-conscious?”

Warren nodded.

“After drinking he seemed to change. He was louder, more extroverted, talking to people that he didn’t know. As the night progressed he became meaner and more cynical. It wasn’t pretty.”

Vincent watched the flame.

“In vino veritas.”

The phrase made Warren think of a night out with his friends a long time ago. They ended up outside a fast food place, talking to some girls that were as drunk as they were. One of them came out with something like, when people drink, the truth comes out.

“He always seems cheerful when he’s sober.”

Vincent looked at Warren.

“Too cheerful. It’s not that he is fake, it just feels a bit unnatural, like he doesn’t want to bring people down.”

Warren looked at the sky.

“What are you like, when you, drink?”

Vincent moved closer to the light.

“The same but my brain slows down.”

The moon was almost full.

“No repressed feelings?”

Vincent could feel the warmth on his face.

“I’m not that bothered by what people think of me therefore no repressed feelings. If I feel like doing something, I find a way of doing it.”

Vincent paused.

“In an email, a girl once told me that she always has one or two drinks before going out to reduce her anxiety. Then she asked if I thought that that was pathetic.”

Warren’s throat felt dry.

“What did you say?”

Vincent watched the flame again.

“Alcohol distorts your perception of reality and that I try to keep mine clear at all times.”

Warren picked up his glass of water.

“Smooth.”

Vincent smiled.

“I asked her to go to a party with me and she said that she wasn’t ready to date yet. I responded by saying that it wasn’t a date and that I didn’t think that that was such a good idea because our ideas about life we’re very different. Being an astute philosophy graduate, she wanted to know what I meant in more depth and then things imploded.”

Vincent picked up the match box.

“The last thing she told me was that she didn’t want to be friends anymore. We haven’t seen or talked to each other in almost ten years. You’ll have to read my novel if you want the details.”

Warren took a sip.

“Why are you smiling?”

Vincent took one out.

“We are animals, we have instincts, we do not choose who or what we are drawn to. When you meet someone that you’re attracted to and you enjoy their company, a switch gets turned on because you feel that there is a potential. You want to get to know them better and in ideal circumstances this happens.”

Vincent slowly moved the sulphurous tip toward the fire.

“However if they have issues with trust, put up a wall, wear a mask and play games. They do not let you get to know them and being rejected by someone that is jaded is funny because, they don’t know you very well, and you don’t know them, so it’s a bit like they don’t really know what they are rejecting.”

The match burst into life.

“It feels like they’re just running away.”

Warren put his glass down.

“I hate philosophy students, they are so pretentious.”

Vincent watched the flame slowly turn the wood black.

“She wasn’t pretentious but she had problems.”

An insect flew by.

“Don’t we all.”
